Motivation and Ideation

Problem Statement

Locomotion

Design a new way to move through the scene and look around, going further than the teleportation and smooth locomotion that most VR experiences fall back on.

Interaction

Give the player full control over an object's position and orientation in 3D space, using something more inventive than a standard grab.

Finding the Idea

Every project starts somewhere, and this one took a few wrong turns before it clicked. Early on, I explored ideas like a wizard locomotion system, drawing spells in the air with a wand to propel yourself through a scene, and an Iron Man style flight mechanic. Both felt promising at first, but had been done before. After attending the first few lectures, there was a clear emphasis on choosing ideas that were genuinely novel.

The real spark came from an unlikely place, a mobile game I'd been completely hooked on at the time, 8 Ball Pool by Miniclip. The core mechanic of lining up a cue, reading the angles, and sending a ball across the table felt like it had potential as a VR locomotion concept. What if the player was the cue ball? Line up a shot, hit it, and travel across the scene.

8 Ball Pool by Miniclip

During the initial lectures, projects of students who took the course in prior years were showcased. Inspired by a miniature-world concept mentioned in one of these lectures, my first instinct was to give the player a small 3D model of the parkour map. They'd line up and hit a shot on this miniature version, and their full-scale avatar would travel accordingly, essentially using the mini table as a movement controller. It was a fun idea, but it added a layer of abstraction that felt unnecessarily complex.

Locomotion Design

After sitting with the miniature table idea for a while, I decided to drop it due to the complexity that comes with having an abstraction layer. Instead, the player simply orbits around their own cue ball in real space and takes a shot directly, just you, the cue, and the ball in front of you. Anyone who's played a VR pool game will find this immediately familiar.

To get a better picture of the concept, I played a few VR pool titles and landed on Black Hole Pool. It is a polished, well-thought-out VR pool experience. It became the primary reference point for how the locomotion mechanic should feel: intuitive cue handling, intuitive shot setup, and responsive ball physics.

Interaction Design

The interaction system went through three distinct iterations before landing on a final approach. Each one revealed a new problem that pushed the design forward.

Iteration 1

The Setter / Rack Idea

The first concept was inspired by the triangle rack used at the start of a pool game. The idea was to have the player fill the rack by placing different balls into it, a satisfying and tactile setup action. The problem? It wasn't novel. At its core, it just meant the player grabs balls with their hands, which is about as standard as VR interaction gets. Scrapped.

Iteration 2

Suction Cup

The second iteration tried to keep the pool theme alive while doing something more interesting. A grab mode would attach a suction cup to the tip of the cue stick, allowing the player to pick up and carry a ball using the stick itself. Translation worked by moving the stick, and rotation was handled by harnessing the two-handed system used to grab the stick. In theory, simple. In practice, controlling object rotation using the stick tip's translation and orientation derived from the two hands proved to be finicky and restrictive.

Iteration 3

Grab Field

The final approach refined the grab mode and deliberately stepped back from strict pool theming in favour of something that actually works well. Rather than a suction cup, the tip of the cue emits a grab field, a zone that locks a nearby object onto the stick. Rotation is split across two input methods: rotating the stick itself handles one axis, while the controller joystick covers the remaining two. Translation is still handled by moving the stick with your hands. The result is a system that gives the player precise, comfortable control over both position and orientation.
