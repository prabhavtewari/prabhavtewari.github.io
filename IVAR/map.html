Modifying the Map

Starting Point

The course provides a template Unity project that serves as a base to build on. It implements a Superman-esque flying locomotion with basic grab interaction. The scene contains a map built from square tiles arranged in a grid, using prefabs for road tiles that form the parkour you have to navigate.

The goal was to modify this map to fit the pool theme, not just aesthetically but functionally. The roads have very shallow curbs and no railings for a ball to rebound off. There is a railing in parts of the map, but only on the bridge prefab that spans the middle section. For the pool mechanics to work, proper railings along both sides of every road section were needed.

Template Project

Building the Railings

Three approaches were tried before arriving at a solution that was both functional and visually acceptable. Each one exposed a different constraint.

Iteration 1

Modifying the Prefab Geometry in Blender

The most direct solution would have been to modify the existing road mesh of the road tile directly in Blender. With no prior Blender experience, this was attempted anyway. After spending a few hours trying to understand the toolset and navigate the existing mesh, it became clear that the skill level required was far beyond what could be picked up quickly. Abandoned.

Iteration 2

Cubes on Prefabs

Since the road is made of prefabs, the idea was to attach simple cubes within unity, to both sides of each road prefab and scale them to span the length of the tile. With careful placement, this produced a decent-looking railing on straight road sections. The problem surfaced with the other prefab types: inclining roads, declining roads, and curved roads all needed their own treatment. Curved roads in particular were a serious obstacle. The curves are smooth, not sharp, meaning a large number of cubes would be needed to approximate a curved railing without it looking jagged. That ruled out cubes as a general solution.

Iteration 2a

Splines for Railings of Curved Roads

Unity's spline tool can be used to generate curved geometry directly inside the editor, which is exactly what was needed for the curved prefabs. Spline-based railings were created for each curved road tile and the results were usable. However, a persistent problem remained: noticeable discontinuities appeared at the seams where splines from one tile met the cubes or splines of the adjacent tile. After spending many hours trying to align the railings smoothly at these junctions, the result was never clean enough.

The seam alignment issue was the deciding factor in moving on. Matching endpoints precisely across independently placed prefabs was too fragile to be practical at any scale.

Discontinuities at Spline Junctions

Iteration 3

Custom Road Built Entirely with Splines

Rather than retrofitting railings onto existing prefabs, the entire road layout was rebuilt using splines. The initial hope was to scale the road spline radially to produce a railing offset, but spline scaling does not behave radially and introduced deviations from the intended path. Instead, the outer and inner edges of the newly created road were traced manually to form the railing geometry. This gave full control over the shape at every point and eliminated the seam problem entirely. Unity is a game engine, not a 3D modelling tool, but for this use case its spline system proved more than capable.

Map Created With Splines

Map Layout

The template project came with a locomotion technique that was quite fast-paced. The pool locomotion is slower by comparison, which made the original map feel too lengthy to navigate comfortably.

The map produced from Iteration 3 was built before the pool locomotion was fully implemented. Once the final locomotion system was in place and playtested, it confirmed the map needed to be shorter. The layout was revised and shortened into a more compact course.

Texture and Physics

With the geometry in place, a pool table felt texture was sourced from TextureCan and applied across the map surface. This gave the road the green baize look needed to complete the pool table aesthetic.

The physics properties of the map play an important role in how the ball rebounds and rolls. Separate physics materials were assigned to the base surface and the railings, each tuned to produce the right amount of friction and bounciness for the pool mechanic to feel right.

Out of Bounds

If the ball falls off the track, it needs to trigger an out-of-bounds event and respawn at the last valid position. Two approaches were tried.

Iteration 1

Spline Mesh Trigger

The railing spline mesh is duplicated and shifted upward to create a grill structure. The top is then capped with a duplicate of the base spline, forming an enclosed volume intended to act as a trigger collider. However, Unity's convex mesh collider is limited to fewer than 255 triangles, a PhysX constraint that applies whenever Is Convex is enabled, which is required for any mesh to act as a trigger. The grill mesh is far too complex for this simplification, and the resulting collider deviated enough from the actual track geometry to make out-of-bounds detection unreliable. Abandoned.

Boundary Definition With Splines

Iteration 2

Sphere and Plane

The second approach used two simple primitives instead of the mesh. A quad hovers slightly above the floor of the scene, covering all space outside the parkour track. A sphere encloses the entire playable area to catch cases where the ball flies completely off the map. Together, the quad and sphere form a playable hemisphere region. Both are set as triggers with their renderers disabled so they remain invisible during play.
