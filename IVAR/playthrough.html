User Study

A small user study was conducted alongside the playtesting. The full response data can be found here. Both participants were experienced VR users, which makes the mixed results more telling than they might first appear. Locomotion understanding, shot control, and rotation all averaged 3.5, pointing to the control complexity flagged elsewhere. Object pickup scored highest at 4.5, suggesting the grab field was the most intuitive part of the system. The sharpest disagreement was on perceived speed, where one user rated it 5 and the other 2, most likely a reflection of shot quality rather than the system itself. Motion sickness fared better than expected given the feedback from the presentation, averaging 4.5.

Feedback

Beyond the user study, the project was presented to coursemates and the professor during the final lecture of the semester. Several recurring themes came up across both the informal testing and the presentation feedback.

Issue 1

Perceived Speed

Several users felt the game was slow-paced. While not conclusively verified, the most likely cause is shot quality rather than the system itself. The ball's speed depends directly on how hard and how centrally it is struck, and an off-centre hit results in noticeably less forward momentum. This is an inherent part of pool mechanics, but without a clear visual cue indicating shot quality, it read as sluggishness. This was also reflected in the user study results.

Issue 2

Cyber Sickness

Cyber sickness was also a commonly reported issue. The surrounding environment, with its vibrant colours and densely packed buildings close to the track, likely contributed to visual discomfort. The rapid acceleration that follows a hard shot also proved to be a source of nausea for some players. Toning down the visual environment and smoothing out peak acceleration would be the most direct ways to address this.

Issue 3

Control Complexity

While the goal was intuitive controls, the design was shaped largely by familiarity with other VR pool titles. For players without that reference point, or those less comfortable with VR controllers in general, the full control scheme turned out to be quite involved. Banners with a complete control mapping were placed at several points along the map in an attempt to help, but onboarding through signage only goes so far.

Issue 4

Hidden System Constraints

Several safeguards built into the system to prevent bugs ended up surfacing as friction for users. Anchor mode can only be activated once the ball has come to a complete stop, which was put in place to avoid the complexity of tracking velocities relative to a moving ball and a player following it. Similarly, anchor mode is blocked if the cue tip is already inside the ball. The grab field has the same pattern: the object only attaches if it lies in front of the attach point at the moment of contact, so approaching from the wrong angle produces no response. All three constraints are technically sound, but none were made explicit to the player, making the system feel unresponsive in moments where the reason was not obvious.
