User Study

The user study was kept deliberately short. Eight questions covering the core systems on the Likert scale, plus two open-ended questionswere asked to reduce friction and encourage honest responses from participants who had just finished playing. Three participants completed it digitally, though only two of their runs are captured in the score table above. The complete responses can be found here.

The results were mixed but largely encouraging. Locomotion understanding was the clearest split, averaging 3.67, some participants found the orbit and shot controls intuitive while others did not, a divide explored further in the section below. The remaining scores tell a more positive story, shot control, perceived speed, object rotation, and motion sickness all averaged 4.0, and object pickup and presence both came in at 4.33. Taken together, users felt broadly comfortable with the interaction system, and the environment held up as convincing for the theme of the project.

The motion sickness scores stand in direct contrast to the classroom demo, where it was one of the most commonly reported complaints. This is not simply a matter of VR experience. Experienced players at the classroom demo reported discomfort too. One possible explanation, though it remains an assumption, is that the classroom demo involved participants switching between multiple different VR applications in realtively quick succession, each with its own locomotion style, whereas the user study sessions were isolated to this application alone. Accumulated disorientation from those back-to-back context switches may have lowered the sickness threshold for classroom demo attendees.

Classroom Demo Feedback

The project was presented to coursemates and the professor during the final lecture of the semester. Several recurring themes came up across the informal observations made there and in the user study.

Issue 1

Perceived Speed

Several users felt the game was slow-paced. While not conclusively verified, the most likely cause is shot quality rather than the system itself. The ball's speed depends directly on how hard and how centrally it is struck, and an off-centre hit results in noticeably less forward momentum. This is an inherent part of pool mechanics, but without a clear visual cue indicating shot quality, it read as sluggishness. This can also be inferred form the user study results.

Issue 2

Cyber Sickness

Cyber sickness was among the most commonly reported issues at the classroom demo, though it did not surface as strongly in the user study scores, as discussed above. The underlying risk factors are still present in the system. The vibrant colours and densely packed buildings close to the track, coupled with the rapid acceleration following a hard shot, likely contributed to the visual discomfort and nausea reported by some players. Toning down the visual environment and smoothing out peak acceleration would be the most direct ways to address this.

Issue 3

Control Complexity

While the goal was to have intuitive controls, the design was shaped largely by familiarity with other VR pool titles. For players without that reference point, or those less comfortable with VR controllers in general, the full control scheme turned out to be quite involved. Banners with a complete control mapping were placed at several points along the map in an attempt to help, but onboarding through signage only goes so far.

Issue 4

Hidden System Constraints

Several safeguards built into the system to prevent bugs ended up surfacing as friction for users. Anchor mode can only be activated once the ball has come to a complete stop, which was put in place to avoid the complexity of tracking velocities relative to a moving ball and a player following it. Similarly, anchor mode is blocked if the cue tip is already inside the ball. The grab field has the same pattern, the object only attaches if it lies in front of the attach point at the moment of contact, so approaching from the wrong angle produces no response. All these constraints are technically sound, but none were made explicit to the player, making the system feel unresponsive in moments where the reason was not obvious. Some users were able to infer these mechanics quickly through trial and error, but for others the behaviour remained opaque throughout the session.
