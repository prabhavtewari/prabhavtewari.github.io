Locomotion System

Orbit Controls

The player orbits around the cue ball to line up a shot. A script on the XR Origin updates its position every frame based on the ball's world position and two orbital parameters: angle and radius. A calculated height offset is applied from the center of the ball so that everyone sits at the same vertical position relative to the ball regardless of their real-world height, giving a uniform experience across players.

The right joystick controls the orbit. Up and down adjust the distance to the ball, left and right rotate around it in the plane parallel to the ground. A deadzone on the joystick input prevents both axes from activating simultaneously, keeping the controls deliberate.

Orbit Controls

Only the XR Origin is locked to the ball. The player can still look around freely by moving their head, and physical movement in the real world carries over into the scene. Locking this out entirely would have felt unnatural.

Anchor Mode

Anchor mode captures the last known position of the anchor hand and holds it fixed. The cue continues to point from the striking hand toward that frozen point, so the stroke stays on a consistent line regardless of where the anchor hand drifts afterward. This turns the shot into a guided linear stroke rather than a freehand swing, making aiming significantly more stable. The idea was directly inspired by how other VR pool titles handle shot setup.

Activating anchor mode also enables the cue's collider, which is kept off at all other times to prevent accidental ball contact while the player is positioning their hands. Anchor mode only activates when the grip button on the anchor hand is pressed and the tip of the cue is outside the ball at that moment.

Striking the Ball

With anchor mode active, the player moves their striking hand back and forth to drive the cue through the anchor point and into the ball. On contact, force is applied to the ball based on the cue's velocity at the moment of impact, and anchor mode is immediately disabled. 

void OnCollisionEnter(Collision collision)
{
    if (!collision.gameObject.CompareTag("CueBall")) return;

    Rigidbody ballRb = collision.rigidbody;

    if (ballRb != null && collision.contactCount > 0 && !hasStruckThisAnchor)
    {
        hasStruckThisAnchor = true;
        Vector3 stickVelocity = manager.calculatedStickVelocity;
        Vector3 forceVector = stickVelocity.normalized * (stickVelocity.magnitude * strikeMultiplier);
        ballRb.AddForce(forceVector, ForceMode.VelocityChange);
        manager.PlayStrikeSound();
    }
}

Getting the collision right took more debugging than expected. With Is Trigger enabled, the event fires but no contact geometry is passed. With it disabled, Unity's physics engine treats the collision as a standard dynamic interaction and automatically transfers momentum from the cue to the ball, causing it to move even without any scripted force.

The fix was to make the cue's Rigidbody kinematic. A kinematic body is still fully tracked by the physics system and continues to fire OnCollisionEnter, but the engine no longer applies automatic impulses from it onto other dynamic bodies. The ball's movement becomes purely script-driven, giving precise control over exactly how much force is applied and when.

After the shot, the ball slows gradually through friction from the felt surface physics material. Pressing Y zeroes the ball's velocity immediately if the player does not want to wait it out.
